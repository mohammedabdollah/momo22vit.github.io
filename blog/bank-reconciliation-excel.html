Stop Wasting Hours: How to Automate Bank Reconciliation in Excel with Power Query

Monthly bank reconciliation is a critical accounting task, but it's often a manual, error-prone headache. Sorting through bank statements, ticking off entries against your general ledger... it can take hours, if not days. But what if you could automate 80% of this process using a powerful tool already built into Excel?

Enter Power Query. This often-overlooked feature is a data transformation engine that can turn your manual reconciliation process into a streamlined, semi-automated workflow. In this guide, I'll walk you through the exact steps to build a powerful reconciliation template that will save you time and improve accuracy, month after month.

The Problem: Why Manual Reconciliation is Inefficient

Before we build the solution, let's quickly define the problem. A typical manual process involves:

  • Visually scanning two different datasets (bank statement and company's cash book).
  • Manually sorting columns and using filters.
  • Risking human error by missing a transaction or matching the wrong amounts.
  • It's not scalable. As transaction volume grows, the time spent on reconciliation explodes.

What is Power Query? Your New Best Friend

Power Query is Excel's data connectivity and preparation tool. Think of it as an "Extract, Transform, Load" (ETL) tool. You can pull in data from almost any source, clean and reshape it with a series of repeatable steps, and load it into an Excel sheet. The best part? Once you set up the steps, you can just click "Refresh" next month, and it does all the work for you.

Step-by-Step: Building Your Automated Reconciliation Model

Ready to build? Let’s get started.

Step 1: Prepare Your Data

You need two data tables to start, formatted as official Excel Tables (select your data and press Ctrl + T):

  1. Bank_Statement: A table with your bank transactions. It should include at least a Date, Description, and Amount column.
  2. General_Ledger: A table with your cash book transactions. It should also have a Date, Description, and Amount.

Step 2: Load Both Tables into Power Query

  • Select the Bank_Statement table.
  • Go to the Data tab in Excel -> From Table/Range.
  • The Power Query Editor will open. Your table is now a "query." Rename the query on the right side to BankData.
  • Click Close & Load To... -> select Only Create Connection. Click OK.
  • Repeat the exact same process for your General_Ledger table, naming the query LedgerData.

Step 3: Clean and Standardize Your Data

This is where the magic begins. Inside the Power Query editor for both queries (BankData and LedgerData), perform any necessary cleaning steps. For example:

  • Remove extra columns: Right-click the header of any column you don't need and select "Remove."
  • Ensure Amount is a number: Check that the Amount column has a "1.2" or "$" icon (meaning it's a number/currency type).
  • Trim text: Select the Description column, go to Transform -> Format -> Trim to remove any accidental spaces.

Every step you take is recorded and will be re-applied automatically every time you refresh.

Your New Workflow

Next month, you don't need to repeat these steps. Your new workflow is:

  1. Paste the new bank statement data into your Bank_Statement table.
  2. Add your new cash book data to the General_Ledger table.
  3. Go to the Data tab and click Refresh All.
  4. All your reports—cleared, outstanding checks, deposits in transit—will update instantly.

Conclusion

By investing a small amount of time to set up this Power Query workflow, you've moved from a tedious manual task to a powerful, automated system. You've reduced the risk of errors and freed up valuable hours that can be spent on analysis and strategic decision-making. Power Query is just one example of how modern accountants can leverage technology to work smarter, not harder.
